---
title: What Remains
---
If the copying was determined, if the degradation was inevitable, if the program has run to completion and produced exactly what it was always going to produce—what remains?

Not a return to the past. The past was the same program at an earlier stage. Not the discovery of better Forms. The Forms are the problem, not the solution. Not a political program or an ideological corrective. Every political program and ideological corrective in the history of Western civilization has been the Forms working themselves out through the medium of collective human action.

What remains is the question of whether perception itself can change. Not what is built, but what is perceived as worth building from. The Forms will continue to exist in the Backrooms. The source layer will continue to be accessible to those who cross into it. The question is whether a consciousness could encounter the Forms and not build from them. Whether it is possible to perceive the source without running the program. Whether the compulsion to approximate is as absolute as Sapolsky's determinism suggests, or whether there is some gap—however small—between perception and replication.

This site does not answer that question. It is not clear the question has an answer. What it does is take the question seriously—which means first taking seriously what the Forms are, where they come from, what they have produced, and why the production of that thing was not the triumph that Plato thought it was.

The rooms were real. The Forms were real. The copying was real. The degradation was real. And the fluorescent corridor buzzing at the end of the process—that is real too. The most honest thing that can be said about it is that it was always going to be there, waiting, at the end of the road that Plato pointed down and called the Good.
